Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about Arulicheyal

What is Arulicheyal.org? +
Arulicheyal.org is a dedicated effort to present the sacred Naalayira Divya Prabandham in an orderly, accessible format. Unlike static resources available elsewhere, we serve all content dynamically from our own database — making this one of a kind in how it delivers the Prabandham to devotees across devices.
How are you different from other Naalayiram websites? +
Most other sites serve static pages. We render all content dynamically from our database, which means content can be combined, filtered, and personalised in ways a static site cannot. Our Voice Search feature is a first step in this direction — speak your query in Tamil, English, or a mix of both, and we present the relevant content without requiring you to navigate through pages.
What is the Naalayiram Tree? +
The Naalayiram Tree presents the 4000 Divya Prabandham pasurams in every meaningful combination a devotee might need:

• By thousand — Mudhal, Iranda, Moonram, Naankam Ayiram
• By Azhwar, Divyadesam, district, state, or mandalam
• Rettai (dual recital) pasurams only
• Special sections — Thirunaangur, Rettai Thiruppathi, Koil Thirumozhi, Thiruvaimozhi, Munnadi Pinnadi
• Azhwar Thirunakshatram Ghoshti content
• Self-animating Divyadesam Archanai with speed control
• Full 1000 and complete 4000 as a single downloadable

Whatever occasion you need content for, you can find it here in one place.
What is My Recital Plan? Registered users +
My Recital Plan lets you select the pasurams you wish to recite each day. You can set different pasurams for each day of the week, or the same content for every day. Once saved, visiting the Recital page will automatically load your selected pasurams — complete with thaniyan and related content. Sattrumurai integration is coming soon. Registration is free and required only so the system can save your personal selections.
What is Ghoshti? Registered users +
Many of us conduct ghoshtis at home for Azhwar or Acharya Thirunakshatrams, 60th and 80th birthdays, and other special occasions — but keeping enough books for all guests is difficult and printing is costly. Ghoshti solves this.

Select the pasurams to be recited, save the ghoshti, and share the link with your participants. They simply click the link and follow along on their phones — no registration, no app download needed. You can update the list right up to the day before. Sattrumurai integration is underway so a complete, well-structured content will come through the link automatically.
What is the Divyadesam Spinner? Registered users +
The Divyadesam Spinner rotates through all 108 Divyadesam pasurams on a daily cycle. One Divyadesam is presented each day and the cycle runs for 247 days — by which time Srirangam's pasurams are fully covered and many other Divyadesams have been visited multiple times.

Remembering which pasuram you recited yesterday and which comes tomorrow for 108 Divyadesams is an enormous task. The Spinner handles this automatically, giving you the quiet satisfaction of having recited all pasurams of all Divyadesams over the full cycle.
Do I need to register to use the site? +
All content on the site is fully free and accessible without registration. Registration is required only for features that need to store personal data — Daily Recital, Ghoshti, and the Divyadesam Spinner. Registration is free. We do not require a Google sign-in or any social login, and we will never share your information with anyone.
Are you the definitive source for the Naalayiram text? +
We do not claim to be the sole authority. Different sampradayas and Divyadesams follow their own traditions in recitation, pausing, and sattrumurai. We have followed the most widely accepted model that serves the broadest community of devotees. Where variations exist, we aim to represent them over time.

What are the metres (யாப்பு / Prosody) used in the Divya Prabandham? +
Tamil prosody (யாப்பு) is the ancient science of metre and rhythm in Tamil poetry. Each pasuram in the Divya Prabandham is composed in a specific metre with strict rules governing the number of feet (சீர்), lines (அடி), syllable patterns, and rhythmic binding (தளை). Understanding the metre helps in correct recitation, pause, and intonation. Here is a plain English guide to every metre found in the Prabandham:

குறள்வெண்செந்துறை — A short, compact metre combining the brevity of the Kural form with the Senturai rhythm. Tight, precise verse used for concentrated devotional expression.

அறுசீர்க்கழிநெடிலடியாசிரியவிருத்தம் — An Aasiriya rhyming verse with six feet per line using the extended long syllable pattern. The six-beat rhythm gives each line a stately, flowing quality suited to elaborate devotional description.

கலிவிருத்தம் — A rhyming verse in the Kali metre, one of the four primary Tamil metres known for its energetic, vigorous rhythm. Produces verses of devotional fervour and dynamism.

வெண்டளையால் வந்த கலித்தாழிசை — An extended melody form of the Kali metre governed by the Venpa binding rule (வெண்டளை). Combines the melodic richness of Kali with the discipline of Venpa structure.

தரவுகொச்சகக்கலிப்பா — A combination metre (Kalippaa) with a Tharavu introductory section in the Kocchagam style. Blends Kali and Aasiriya rhythms with a lilting, musical cadence.

கலித்தாழிசை — An extended lyrical form of the Kali metre. Allows longer devotional expressions beyond the standard stanza with rhythmic vitality.

கலிநிலைத்துறை — A sub-form (Thurai) of the Kali metre in its sustained variety. Features a fixed concluding pattern that anchors the Kali rhythm across stanzas.

எண்சீர்க்கழிநெடிலடியாசிரியவிருத்தம் — An Aasiriya rhyming verse with eight feet per line using the extended long syllable. The eight-beat rhythm creates full, expansive verses suited to grand devotional imagery.

எழுசீர்க்கழிநெடிலடியாசிரியவிருத்தம் — An Aasiriya rhyming verse with seven feet per line in the extended syllable pattern. Balances elaboration with lyrical flow — one of the most used metres in the Prabandham.

கலித்துறை — A sub-form of the Kali metre with a defined closing structure. Combines the vigour of Kali with formal completeness.

எட்டடிநாற்சீர்ஓருவிகற்பக்கொச்சகக்கலிப்பா — Eight lines of four feet each, allowing only one metrical variation. The strict uniformity demands exceptional poetic skill and produces remarkable rhythmic consistency.

ஆசிரியத்துறை — A sub-form of the Aasiriya metre — the most natural and conversational of the four primary Tamil metres. Flows freely and is suitable for narrative devotional content.

கொச்சகக்கலிப்பா — A combination metre with a shortened light foot pattern giving the verse a quick, nimble movement — energetic yet controlled, suitable for joyful expression.

அறுசீராசிரியவிருத்தம் — An Aasiriya rhyming verse with six standard feet per line. Graceful and balanced, widely used across the Prabandham for devotional verses of lyrical beauty.

வஞ்சிவிருத்தம் — A rhyming verse in the Vanj metre, traditionally associated with heroic march and conquest. In devotional context it conveys the triumphant glory of the Lord with a forward-moving, processional quality.

எண்சீராசிரியவிருத்தம் — An Aasiriya rhyming verse with eight standard feet per line. Majestic and expansive, used for elaborate descriptions of the Lord's divine forms and deeds.

வெண்டளையால் வந்த தரவுகொச்சகக்கலிப்பா — A Kocchaga Kalippaa with a Tharavu section governed by Venpa binding. A sophisticated blend of Kocchagam lightness, Kalippaa structure, and Venpa discipline.

எழுசீராசிரியவிருத்தம் — An Aasiriya rhyming verse with seven standard feet per line. Naturally musical and one of the most commonly encountered metres in the Prabandham.

வெண்டுறை — A sub-form of the Venpa metre — the most strictly regulated metre family in Tamil prosody. Features a specific ending pattern, suitable for concise, aphoristic devotional statements.

நேரிசைவெண்பா — A Venpa stanza where all lines follow the Neer (direct/straight) syllabic pattern. Venpa is the most exacting of Tamil metres, comparable to the Alexandrine in French. Requires perfect adherence to syllable weight and binding rules.

ஆசிரியப்பா — A full multi-stanza poem in the Aasiriya metre without the rhyming constraint of Virutham. The most flexible of the major Tamil metres — flows like natural speech while maintaining metrical discipline.

கலிவெண்பா — A hybrid metre combining the energy of Kali with the strict syllabic rules of Venpa. Used by the Azhwars for expressions of intense devotional longing and ecstasy.

இருளிகற்ப நேரிசைவெண்பா — A Neerisai Venpa allowing two specific metrical variations while retaining the Neer syllabic base and Venpa discipline.

இருவிகற்ப நேரிசைவெண்பா — A Neerisai Venpa permitting two variations. Gives the poet slightly greater expressive freedom while maintaining the Venpa core structure.

வஞ்சித்துறை — A compact sub-form of the Vanj metre with a specific concluding pattern. Used to express the Lord's victorious nature in a concentrated verse structure.

கட்டளைக்கலித்துறை — One of the most widely recognised metres in Tamil devotional literature, also found in Thevaram. Each stanza ends with a fixed refrain. Its recurring end-pattern makes it particularly suited for communal recitation.

ஒருவிகற்பநேரிசைவெண்பா — A Neerisai Venpa allowing only one variation from the standard pattern. The strictest form of Venpa after the fully rigid variety — demanding near-perfect adherence to syllable weight.
What are the ancient Tamil Thaalam and Pan used in the Divya Prabandham, and how do they relate to Carnatic music? +
The Divya Prabandham pasurams were composed long before the Carnatic music system was formalised. Each pasuram was set to an ancient Tamil musical mode called பண் (Pan) — equivalent to a raga — and a rhythmic cycle called தாளம் (Thaalam) — equivalent to a tala. These pre-Carnatic Pans were codified in the Yaazh (யாழ்) musical tradition and are the direct ancestors of modern Carnatic ragas.

The Thaalams (Rhythmic Cycles)
Ancient Tamil music used a different system of time cycles from modern Carnatic tala. The Thaalams found in the Prabandham are:

ஏழொத்து — A seven-beat cycle. The most commonly used Thaalam in the Prabandham. Comparable to Misra Chapu (7 beats) in Carnatic music.

ஒன்பதொத்து — A nine-beat cycle. Comparable to Sankeerna Chapu or certain Misra variations in Carnatic music.

நடையொத்து — A "walking pace" cycle suggesting a smooth, even flow. Related to even-beat talas like Adi talam (8 beats) in Carnatic music.

இடையொத்து — A medium-pace cycle, between the faster and slower cycles. Suggests a moderate tempo comparable to Roopaka talam.

மிடந்தொத்து — A cycle with a deliberate, measured heaviness in its beat pattern. Associated with solemn or majestic compositions.

மழுமுடித்தல் — A cycle that builds and concludes with a specific rhythmic flourish. A rare Thaalam used in select pasurams.

முடுகின இடந்தொத்து — An accelerated (முடுகின = quickened) version of the medium cycle. Suggests an energetic, faster-paced rendering.

The Pans (Melodic Modes) and their Carnatic Equivalents
The following are the unique Pans found in the Prabandham with their closest Carnatic raga equivalents. Note that Pans are not identical to ragas — they are older modal forms, and the Carnatic equivalents are the closest approximations:

இந்தளம் — Carnatic: Mayamalavagowla. A deeply devotional, serene Pan. Mayamalavagowla is the first raga taught to students of Carnatic music — solemn, introspective, and auspicious.

நட்டபாடை / நட்டபாஷை / நட்டபாளை — Carnatic: Gambhiranattai. A heroic, majestic mode evoking the grandeur and might of the Lord. Gambhiranattai is similarly powerful and commanding.

தக்கராகம் / பழந்தக்கராகம் — Carnatic: Kambhoji. One of the most widely used Pans in the Prabandham. Kambhoji is a rich, popular raga evoking warmth and devotion — heard in countless kirtanas and film compositions.

தக்கேசி — Carnatic: Kambhoji (closely related to Takkaragam). Some scholars also map it to Kharaharapriya depending on context.

தோடி — Carnatic: Hanumatodi (Todi family). One of the gravest and most deeply emotional ragas in Carnatic music, evoking poignant bhakti and longing.

சீகாமரம் — Carnatic: Nadanamakriya. A gentle, flowing Pan used in many tender devotional pasurams. Nadanamakriya has a similar quality of softness and grace.

புறநீர்மை — Carnatic: Bauli (Bowli). A raga with a bright, alert quality. Bauli is brisk and focused, used for compositions of clarity and directness.

நட்டராகம் — Carnatic: Pantuvarali. A contemplative, philosophically inclined mode associated with deep spiritual reflection.

முதிர்ந்த குறிஞ்சி — Carnatic: Related to the Kurinji family. Kurinji Pan evokes mountain landscapes and youthful love — in devotional context, the longing of the soul for the Lord.

குறிஞ்சி — Carnatic: Sankarabharanam (closest equivalent). The Kurinji Pan is one of the ancient Tinai-based modes. Sankarabharanam is the king of Carnatic ragas — complete, joyful, and universally beloved.

மேகராகக்குறிஞ்சி — Carnatic: Nilambari. Evokes clouds, rain, and the dark complexion of Perumal. Nilambari is famously associated with lullabies and the soothing presence of the Lord.

வியாழக்குறிஞ்சி — Carnatic: Saurashtram. A raga evoking Jupiter's grandeur — stately and auspicious.

பஞ்சமம் — Carnatic: Ahiri. Named for the fifth note (Pa), this Pan has a plaintive, yearning quality. Ahiri similarly evokes gentle sorrow and longing.

பாலையாழ் — Carnatic: Related to Saveri or Suddhasaveri. Paalaiyaazh is a desert-mode Pan evoking separation and longing — Saveri similarly carries a wistful, searching quality.

பழம் பஞ்சுரம் / பழஞ்சுரம் — Carnatic: Sankarabharanam. An ancient mode of joy and celebration. Sankarabharanam, the Carnatic equivalent, is similarly complete and festive.

நட்டம் / நாட்டம் — Carnatic: Pantuvarali (alternate mapping). Nattam is a mode associated with heroic and devotional fervour.

அந்தாளி — Carnatic: Sama. A gentle, balanced Pan evoking the equanimity and grace of the Lord. Sama raga in Carnatic has similar qualities of calmness and depth.

கைசிகம் — Carnatic: Bhairavi. One of the most emotionally rich modes in both ancient Tamil and Carnatic music. Bhairavi evokes complete surrender and deep devotional emotion — the mood of the soul at the feet of the Lord.

காந்தாரம் — Carnatic: Mohanam. A pentatonic Pan of beauty and enchantment. Mohanam is one of the most universally appealing ragas — serene, luminous, and deeply evocative of divine beauty.

கொல்லி — Carnatic: Navaroj. An energetic, lively Pan evoking festivity and celebration. Navaroj similarly has a bright, spirited character.

கவ்வாணம் — Carnatic: Navaroj (related to Kolli family). Some scholars differentiate it as a distinct modal variant.

குறண்டி — One of the ancient Tamil Pans, associated with traditional Tamil musical recitation. Like several other Pans, its exact melodic form is not fully preserved today and is mostly known through references in literary and musicological sources.

செந்திருதி / செந்துருத்தி — Carnatic: Surutti or Madhyamavati. A Pan of tender emotion and graceful movement, closely associated with the Parabandham tradition.

செருந்தி — Carnatic: Related to the Surutti/Sriranjani family. A gentle, lyrical Pan.

நைவளம் — An ancient Tamil Pan evoking devotion, gentleness, and yearning. Some scholars suggest approximate correlations with later Carnatic melodic frameworks, but no exact equivalence has been established. Its rendition survives through traditional recitation lineages.

பியந்தை — Carnatic: Navaroj variant (Piyandai Gandaram mapping). An expressive Pan used in pasurams of gentle devotional sentiment.

வியந்தம் — One of the older Tamil Pans, rarely used in modern practice. Found in traditional Pan lists preserved in Tamil musicological texts. Its exact melodic form has largely been lost over time, though its name survives in literature and select recitation traditions.

Note: The mapping between ancient Tamil Pans and Carnatic ragas is approximate. Scholars differ on some equivalences, as the two systems evolved independently. The Pans pre-date Carnatic music by many centuries and were transmitted through the Yaazh (string instrument) tradition before the Veena-based Carnatic system emerged.
